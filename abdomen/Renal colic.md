## ðŸ’Š Pain Management

- Offer a **non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drug (NSAID)** (e.g., diclofenac or ibuprofen), unless contraindicated. NSAIDs are the most effective first-line analgesia for suspected acute renal colic. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}  
- If NSAIDs are contraindicated or not sufficient:
  - Consider **intravenous paracetamol**.
  - Consider an **opioid analgesic** if pain remains uncontrolled. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

## ðŸ©º Imaging

### Adults (Non-Pregnant)

- Offer **urgent low-dose non-contrast CT KUB** (CT of kidneys, ureters and bladder) within **24 hours** of presentation in suspected renal/ureteric colic. :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}  
- CT KUB is the **most accurate imaging** modality and assists in confirming ureteric stones, impact, number, size and complication. :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}

### Pregnant Patients

- Offer **ultrasound** as the first-line imaging test instead of CT to avoid radiation exposure. :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}  
- If ultrasound is inconclusive and clinical suspicion remains, consider specialist imaging discussion (e.g., MRI) in consultation with radiology.

### Children and Young People

- For children and young people, start with **ultrasound** first. If uncertain, consider low-dose non-contrast CT. :contentReference[oaicite:10]{index=10}

## ðŸ›‘ Red Flags and Urgent Referral

Urgent surgical/Urology or on-call admission should be considered in patients with:

- **Uncontrolled or intractable pain**
- **Sepsis or systemic infection**
- **Severe acute kidney injury / anuria**
- **Concern about alternative serious pathology**
- **Unexplained fever with suspected obstructed infected stone (pyonephrosis)** :contentReference[oaicite:11]{index=11}
